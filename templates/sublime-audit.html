The Product

You have something real here.

Beef tallow skincare is a growing category with strong word-of-mouth demand. The science backs it up. The ingredient profile is genuinely different from what's on the shelves at Ulta or Sephora. That is your edge, and it is a significant one.

Your product line is priced well. Most items in the $20-35 range, with entry points as low as $5 and higher-value products over $100. That range gives us everything we need to build a high-converting store with real average order value.

The product is not the problem. The packaging around it is.

The Homepage

The first thing a buyer sees is working against you.

Critical

The banner is creating confusion, not conversions.

"20% Off Your First Order" is a strong hook. But the call to action next to it says "Sign up now." To a buyer, "sign up" does not mean "shop." It means "give me your email before I give you anything." That is the opposite of what a discount banner should do. A buyer sees 20% off, gets excited, and then gets stopped by a signup wall instead of being dropped into the store with the discount already applied. The banner should drive people straight to the product, not into an email capture form.

High Impact

The "Learn" link in the navigation is sending the wrong message.

When a buyer sees "Learn" in the nav bar, the subconscious read is "you won't understand this product without doing homework first." That is not the energy a skincare brand should give off. Nobody browsing Sephora has to click "Learn" before they feel confident enough to buy. A short product description on the product page should do all the educating. That nav slot should be pointing to something that helps people buy, not something that implies they need to study first.

Critical

The website does not match the quality of the product.

Your logo is beautiful. It communicates elegance and confidence. But the website around it does not carry that same energy. Your buyers have shopped on Sephora, Ulta, Glossier. That is the subconscious standard they hold every skincare brand to. The gap between your logo and your site is the single biggest thing holding back conversions. A brand with a worse product but a better website would outsell this store today, and that needs to change.

The Product Pages

Where the actual buying decision happens.

Critical

There is no product description above the fold.

When someone lands on a product page, they see the product name, a size selector, a price, and an Add to Cart button. What they do not see is any explanation of what the product actually does or why they should care. "BEE-Natural Botox" is a clever name, but it does not tell a first-time buyer what they are buying. The product description needs to be visible before the purchase buttons, not buried below the fold. If a buyer has to scroll down to find out what they are looking at, most of them will not scroll.

Critical

Too many competing calls to action on one screen.

On a single product page, there is an Add to Cart button, a Buy with Shop Pay button, a Subscribe and Save option, and a "Get 20% Off" popup floating in the corner. That is four different things asking for the buyer's attention at the same time. When everything is asking for a click, nothing gets clicked. The path from landing to checkout should be one clear decision, not four competing ones. Simplify this down to one primary action and remove everything that creates hesitation.

High Impact

The subscription option is adding friction to first-time buyers.

A "Subscribe and Save" option on the first purchase creates hesitation. A new customer who has never tried your product is being asked to commit to recurring charges before they even know if they like it. Remove this from the initial purchase flow entirely. Let the product speak for itself. Follow up with well-written email sequences after they have tried it, and they will come back on their own. Retention is built through experience, not forced commitment.

High Impact

No social proof anywhere on the site.

There are no reviews, no testimonials, and no user-generated content on any product page. For a brand that most buyers have never heard of, this is a significant trust gap. A first-time buyer needs to see that other real people have tried this product and loved it before they will enter their credit card. Even a handful of genuine reviews with photos will dramatically change the conversion rate. Without social proof, every visitor is making a leap of faith, and most people will not take that leap on a brand they just discovered.

The Brand

The identity is strong but fragmented.

Critical

The brand name is split across three different identities.

The URL says "sublimetallow.com." The logo says "Sublime Apothecary & Co." The Instagram handle is @sublimeapothecary. The copyright in the footer says "Sublime Apothecary." You are already using the better name everywhere except the one place that matters most, the domain. "Tallow" in the URL requires education. "Apothecary" triggers instant familiarity with skincare. This is not a rebrand. This is just finishing what you already started. A domain swap is simple, fast, and will immediately improve click-through rates from every channel.

High Impact

Product photography is inconsistent.

We can see what you are going for with the current images. The intention is there. But the execution is not at the level the product deserves. In skincare, the product photos are the product. Your buyer cannot touch it, smell it, or try it before they buy. The image is everything. Studio-quality product shots, consistent lighting, and a cohesive visual identity across every product page will change how people perceive the entire brand.

Opportunity

No upsell or bundle mechanics in place.

There are no strategic product bundles, no post-checkout upsells, and the product recommendations appear to be random. Upsells should only appear after a buyer has initiated checkout, not before. Let them commit to the decision first, then offer a complementary product at a discount while they are already in buying mode. Even a $5 increase in average order value at scale changes everything. These mechanics are standard on every high-performing ecom store, and they are completely absent here.

Here is the thing that should get you excited: none of this is a product problem. You have a genuinely good product with real demand. Everything holding you back is presentation and conversion mechanics. Fix the store first so it is ready to convert. Then launch a UGC creator campaign that puts your products in front of millions of people. That is the play. All of it is fixable, and most of it is fast.

The Play

What $100K months actually look like for this brand.

Two phases. First, rebuild the store so it converts. Then, launch the campaign that sends it traffic.

Phase 1: Prepare the store
  1. Rebuild the store for conversion.
    Design the entire site around the identity your logo already communicates. High-end, refined, confident. Every page built to move a visitor toward a purchase with the shortest possible path. Product-specific landing pages that take someone from click to checkout in under 3 taps. Clean product descriptions above the fold. One clear call to action per page. Mobile-first, because that is where your buyers are.
  2. Studio-quality product photography.
    Consistent, clean, elevated images across every product. This can be done with 3D rendering and AI-assisted generation to get butter-clean visuals without a $10K photo shoot. The goal is that when someone lands on any product page, the image alone makes them trust the brand.
  3. Build the conversion mechanics into the store.
    Strategic product bundles designed around how the products actually complement each other. Post-checkout upsells that increase average order value without adding friction to the initial decision. One-click checkout initiation from every product page. Remove the subscription option from first purchases. These are the invisible systems that separate a $30K month from a $100K month.
  4. Unify the brand and change the domain.
    Move to sublimeapothecary.com or a similar keyword that your buyers already associate with skincare. Align the URL with what the logo, Instagram, and footer already say. This is a 30-minute change that will improve click-through rates from every single channel, starting immediately.
Phase 2: Send the traffic
  1. Launch a UGC creator campaign. This is the slingshot.
    Once the store is rebuilt and the conversion mechanics are in place, this is how you flood it with high-intent buyers. Reach out to 100-200 creators across TikTok, Instagram, and Facebook. Offer product plus commission on their unique links. Provide them with exactly how the videos need to be made, what to say, what to show, and how to frame the product. Each creator gets a specific product, and every video drives traffic to a product-specific landing page. This is how unknown brands break through the trust barrier overnight. Your buyers will feel like they already know you before they ever visit the site. Phase 1 prepares the store to receive this traffic. This is what sends it.
  2. Email sequences that continue the conversation.
    If someone saw a video about your whipped tallow moisturizer and started checking out but did not finish, the follow-up email three days later needs to pick up exactly where that video left off. Same language, same framing, same energy. When you do this, they think "oh yeah, I wanted that." When you do not, you are starting from zero again and they are already onto the next brand. This is how you recover abandoned carts at a rate that actually moves revenue.
